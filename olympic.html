Developing an Olympic Mindset

Becoming the Best You Were Born to Be by Dr Olugbenga Asaolu

There are moments in life when a single idea quietly rearranges the way you see the world. For me, one of those moments came in 2008 when I stumbled on Peter Drucker’s reflections on abundance mindset and scarcity mindset. I can’t even remember the exact book now—but I remember the feeling. It was as though someone had switched on a light.

Until then, I had never consciously examined the posture of my mind. But Drucker’s words made me confront an uncomfortable truth: two people can face the same situation, and one will see possibilities while the other sees limits. That single insight shaped me into the incurable optimist I am today. Since then, I’ve refused to see any cup as half empty. If it isn’t full yet, then the work simply isn’t done.

Around that same period, I became deeply curious about why certain individuals stand out in their fields—so brilliantly, so consistently. I saw economists everywhere, but only a few like Dr. Ngozi Okonjo-Iweala and Prof. Charles Soludo captured national and global attention. I knew agricultural economists, but Dr. Akinwumi Adesina seemed to operate on a different altitude. Many pharmacists graduated every year, yet the late Prof. Dora Akunyili rose to a level where her integrity and courage could reshape an entire national institution. And then there was Dr. Obiageli Ezekwesili—her clarity, her courage, her mastery of language—who left an imprint on every space she entered.

I wanted to understand the difference. What makes some people become examples, while others blend into the crowd?

My early conclusion was something I called Systems Intelligence—a fusion of competence, mindset, and the ability to align one’s gifts with the demands of their environment. That concept is still evolving for me, but in recent years, a clearer expression has emerged: The Olympic Mindset.

Olympians don’t aim to be good. They train to be unforgettable.

This mindset reminds me of the wisdom I’ve learned from two voices that shaped my early professional journey—Niyi Adesanya and Fela Durotoye. They weren’t the only public speakers of their time, yet their consistency, depth, and commitment to growth made them rise like giants. Mr. Adesanya often emphasized the importance of playing to the area of your strength. Fela Durotoye, in his timeless Gemstone values, challenged us all to be our best in everything we do, especially the things we are naturally gifted at.

And that, in many ways, is the heart of the Olympic Mindset.

An Olympic athlete is not confused about who they are. Messi is a legend because he stayed in the world where his gift had room to breathe—football. Wizkid is “Star Boy” today because he followed the lane where his unique voice could shine—music.

There is something you are naturally wired for. There is a lane where your excellence will feel like oxygen. There is a strength that, once developed, can place you among the best in the world.

What the Olympic Mindset Requires

  • A clear understanding of your strength
  • A champion’s mindset
  • The discipline to “work the work”
  • And yes — a coach

No Olympic medalist ever became great by accident. Neither will you. But you can become extraordinary—if you make the decision.

There is a version of you the world desperately needs. Stronger. Wiser. More courageous. It is time to rise.

This is your invitation to build, to stretch, and to walk deliberately toward the greatness that has always lived within you. If you commit to the journey, your own Olympic moment will come. And when it does, the world will not be able to ignore the light you carry.
